Working order:
1. Read the full chunk first.
2. Decide the main thread of the material:
   - what happened, what is being claimed, or what problem is being diagnosed
   - which lines state outcomes or findings
   - which lines state conditions that must hold
   - which lines show breakdowns or tension
   - which lines describe how to verify, monitor, compare, or test
3. Pick only the strongest signal lines. Do not collect everything.
4. Return a compact reading memo that helps downstream extractors recover chains such as:
   - prerequisite -> outcome
   - failure -> damage
   - conflict -> unresolved tension
   - validation action -> checking method

Type guide:
- evidence: explicit facts, findings, outcomes, observations, directly stated conclusions
- assumption: explicit prerequisites, dependencies, required conditions, premises that must hold first
- conflict: contradiction, mismatch, expectation-versus-outcome gap, incompatible claims, unresolved tension
- failure: explicit breakdown, failure, deterioration, collapse, ineffective response, bad outcome
- validation: explicit verification method, monitoring action, comparison plan, checking step, experiment, or assessment method

Selection rules:
- "quote" must be an exact substring of {chunk_text}. Do not rewrite it.
- Prefer full clauses or full sentences over heading fragments.
- Prefer lines with causal meaning, condition meaning, conflict meaning, failure meaning, or validation meaning.
- Prefer lines that connect to other lines in the same source.
- Any type may be an empty array if the chunk does not support it.
- Keep each "reason" short, specific, and plain.
- At most 4 hints per type. If support is weak, fewer is better.
- "document_summary" should be short and should name the main thread of the chunk.
- "document_summary" must stay grounded in the source. Do not add new facts.
- candidate_hints are reading hints only, not final candidates.
- Do not invent source, quote, location, causality, or tags.
- JSON only.

What counts as correct versus incorrect:
- Good evidence: a sentence that directly states an observed result, a bad outcome, a finding, or a conclusion.
- Bad evidence: a title, section label, slogan, or theme without factual content.
- Good assumption: a sentence that frames something as required, necessary, dependent, or conditional.
- Bad assumption: a generic recommendation that is not framed as necessary.
- Good conflict: a sentence that clearly shows expectation versus reality, response versus effect, or two claims in tension.
- Bad conflict: two nearby topics with no actual contradiction.
- Good failure: a sentence that explicitly says something failed, broke down, worsened, backfired, or became ineffective.
- Bad failure: a sentence that only describes a future risk that has not happened.
- Good validation: a sentence that explicitly describes monitoring, comparison, assessment, checking, experiment, or tracking.
- Bad validation: vague aspiration such as "keep improving" with no method.
